writing style(s)

practical style

[In Practical style] the reader's need, not the writer's desire to articulate something, initiates the writing. The writer's job is to serve the reader's immediate need by delivering timely materials. [...] Since the reader is engaged in solving a problem, the reading is not an end in itself, it is instrumental to some other end.

Thomas and Turner, Clear and Simple as the Truth

practical style

  • concise
  • direct
  • straightforward
  • gets out of the reader's way

why practical style?

  • almost always suitable
  • foundation of good academic writing
  • relatively easy to do

a basic practical workflow

  1. use git
  2. at least two documents: notes and draft
  3. write your first draft without concern for quality
  4. edit it until it is good

getting started

  1. git init.
  2. create notes document.

the notes document

  1. solely for your consumption.
  2. records decisions and information you might need.
  3. records any sources you use (or use bibtex).
  4. holds interesting writing you cut out.

deciding your focus

  • what is your writing about?
  • can you make it more specific?
  • make it more specific!
  • you can generalise later.

beginning your notes document

  1. write down your focus
  2. decide on a tone
  3. write some user personas (see lecture one)
  4. start a concept list (see lecture one)
  5. list main references
  6. write first impressions of each

decide on a tone

  • e.g. formal/informal
  • use of humour?
  • pronouns
    • are you "i" or "we"?
    • do you ever address the reader as "you"?
    • what's the generic 3rd person pronoun? (it's them)

writing a first draft

  • do not edit while drafting!
  • goal is to create anything.
  • do whatever gets you writing.
  • don't worry about quality.
  • don't worry about length.
  • never submit your first draft!

editing

  1. edit for audience
  2. edit for focus
  3. edit for flow
  4. edit for voice
  5. edit for spelling, grammar, etc.

editing for audience

  1. see last lecture.
  2. read through as each user persona.
  3. add confusing things to the concept list.
  4. simplify or insert explanations.

editing for focus

  1. cut things by moving them to notes.
  2. find the beginning and cut everything before that.
  3. find the ending and cut everything after that.
  4. for each sentence ask "why is this here?"
  5. maybe work some bits you cut back in.

On March 27 the Board of Directors met at the Holiday Inn in Podunk. All but two members were present. John Burdic of the Tymecomp Agency presented the results of his time and productivity. [...] the board voted unanimously to put the company on a four-day workweek, effective June 1.

vs

On June 1 the company will go on a four-day work week.

Provost, 100 ways to improve your writing, p34

editing for flow

  1. move important bits earlier in document.
  2. start sentences with things you've introduced.
  3. finish with things you are introducing.
  4. insert bridging sentences.

editing for voice

  1. read your document out loud.
  2. check you have the tone you decided.
  3. fix bits that sound awkward.
  4. insert breaks to create rhythm.

editing for voice

This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety. Now listen. I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony. I use short sentences. And I use sentences of medium length. And sometimes when I am certain the reader is rested, I will engage him with a sentence of considerable length, a sentence that burns with energy and builds with all the impetus of a crescendo, the roll of drums, the crash of the cymbals-sounds that say listen to this, it is important.

So write with a combination of short, medium, and long sentences. Create a sound that pleases the reader's ear. Don't just write words. Write music.
