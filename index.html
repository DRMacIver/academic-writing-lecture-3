what is academic writing?

  1. A writer who is persistent, open-minded, and disciplined.
  2. Writing from a place of reason rather than emotion.
  3. For a rational reader, reading for information.
Irvin, L. Lennie. "What Is “Academic” Writing?."
writingspaces (2010): 3.

what is academic writing?

  1. Writing that is written by academics.
  2. For an audience of academics.
  3. ...who agree that it is academic.

academic writing isn't a thing

  • Disciplines: science, humanities, engineering...
  • Subdisciplines: computer science, physics, biology...
  • Types: books, journals, conferences, essays...
  • Venues: which journal, which conference.

there is no single academic style

style

  • What can be known?
  • What can be put into words?
  • What is the relationship between thought and language?
  • Who is the writer addressing and why?
  • What is the relationship between writer and reader?
  • What are the implied conditions of discourse?
Thomas, Francis-Noël, and Mark Turner.
Clear and simple as the truth: Writing classic prose.
Princeton University Press, 2011.

style: model scenes

  • A model scene is where the discussion is happening.
  • e.g. one person explaining to another, an instructor helping a student, a speech, a debate, and many more.

style of a computer science paper

  • The world can be known through reason and experimentation.
  • All knowledge of interest can be put into words (or symbols).
  • Thought precedes language, but language structures thought.

style of a computer science paper

  • The writer is addressing a community of their peers.
  • The community is collaborating to advance human knowledge.
  • ...and for the advancement of their own academic careers.
  • The audience must be persuaded, but is open to being so.

style of a computer science paper

  • The model scene is a conference.

how to write like a computer scientist

  • Don't. You're not researchers (yet).
  • It's not useful to you and you won't be very good at it.
  • Focus on clear, simple, technical, prose.
  • Understand, then emulate.

how to read computer scientists

what is a paper?

  • Presents an idea.
  • And an argument that said idea is: True, interesting, and useful.

what are papers for?

  • Advancing human knowledge.
  • Pushing an agenda.
  • Self-promotion.

advancing human knowledge.

  • Developing new theory and descriptive tools.
  • Making an analytic argument (mathematics, philosophy).
  • Making an empirical argument that something is true (science).
  • Telling a story.

pushing an agenda.

  • All knowledge is social, so all knowledge is political.
  • This isn't necessarily bad!
  • ...but anyone who says academia is value neutral is lying to you.
  • Minimum viable agenda: This area of study is interesting.

self-promotion.

  • Academics are people too, with a career.
  • We get judged on our papers, and their success.
  • Popular papers lead to power, prestige, and (some) money.
  • Proper attribution of credit is vital to this.

how to read a paper

A paper should be read in three passes:

  1. Get a general idea about the paper (5-10 minutes).
  2. Grasp the content, but not details (up to an hour).
  3. Understand the paper in depth (a few hours).
Keshav, Srinivasan. "How to read a paper."
ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review 37.3 (2007): 83-84.
